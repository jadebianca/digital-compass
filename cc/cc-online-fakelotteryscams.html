Fake Lottery & Prize Scams

By: Hamoy, Kurt Tobey • April 2026

Intro • What Are Lottery Scams • How They Work • Common Variations • Who Gets Targeted • How to Avoid Them • Conclusion

Introduction

"Congratulations! You have won ₱1,000,000! Click here to claim your prize." If you have ever received a message like this, you may have been targeted by a lottery or prize scam. Fake lottery and prize scams are fraudulent schemes that falsely inform victims that they have won a prize, then demand payment of fees or personal information before releasing the "winnings." These scams are among the oldest and most persistent forms of fraud, and they continue to evolve with technology. In the Philippines, thousands of people fall for these schemes every year, losing both money and personal data.

What Are Fake Lottery and Prize Scams?

Fake lottery and prize scams trick victims into believing they have won a large prize in a contest or lottery they never actually entered. The scammer then demands that the victim pay processing fees, taxes, or other charges in order to release the prize — a prize that does not exist. These scams operate through various channels, including text messages, emails, social media platforms, and even physical mail. In the Philippines, the PNP-ACG and the National Telecommunications Commission (NTC) have received many reports of lottery scam texts that appear to come from legitimate sources like telecom companies or government agencies. No legitimate lottery or prize program ever requires winners to pay fees upfront.

How Fake Lottery Scams Work

Lottery scams typically follow a well-established script designed to manipulate the victim through excitement, urgency, and false authority. The scammer first sends a message claiming the victim has won a large amount of money or a valuable prize. The message looks official, often using logos and names of real companies, government agencies, or international organizations. The victim is then instructed to contact a "prize agent" or "claim officer" to begin the release process. The agent then asks for processing fees, legal fees, customs duties, or taxes — all to be paid upfront before the prize can be released.

Common Variations of Lottery and Prize Scams

Lottery and prize scams appear in many different forms, each tailored to seem more believable to a specific audience. SMS lottery scams send mass text messages claiming the recipient has won a prize. Email lottery scams claim to be from international organizations. Social media prize scams use fake posts or ads with celebrity photos. Phone call scams involve a caller guiding the victim through a fake claim process. Physical mail scams send letters with fake checks that bounce after fees are paid. Each variation is designed to lower the victim's guard and make the offer seem as real as possible.

Who Is Most Likely to Be Targeted?

While anyone can be targeted, certain groups are more likely to fall victim. Elderly individuals are frequently targeted because they may be less familiar with digital scams. Individuals experiencing financial difficulties may be more susceptible. People previously victimized are sometimes targeted again. Frequent online contest participants may also believe winning notifications are real.

How to Recognize and Avoid Lottery Scams

If you did not enter, you cannot win. Treat any unsolicited message claiming you won a prize as suspicious. Legitimate prizes never require fees upfront. Do not click on links in prize messages, as they may lead to phishing sites or malware. Verify claims independently, never share personal information, and report suspicious messages to the NTC or PNP-ACG.

Conclusion

Fake lottery and prize scams are persistent forms of fraud. The excitement of winning often overcomes skepticism. Once emotionally invested, victims pay fees in hopes of collecting winnings. No legitimate lottery requires upfront payment. Verify claims independently and educate loved ones to stay safe. When it sounds too good to be true, it always is.
