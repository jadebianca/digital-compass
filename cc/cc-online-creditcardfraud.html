Credit Card Fraud

By: Hamoy, Kurt Tobey • April 2026

Intro • What is Credit Card Fraud • Common Methods • Types of Fraud • Warning Signs • How to Stay Safe • Conclusion

Introduction

Credit cards make buying things convenient, but they also come with a risk — credit card fraud. Credit card fraud is the unauthorized use of someone's credit card or card information to make purchases or withdraw money. It is one of the most prevalent financial crimes in the Philippines and globally. With more Filipinos shopping online and using digital payments, the opportunity for fraud has greatly increased. This article will help you understand how credit card fraud happens and what you can do to protect yourself.

What is Credit Card Fraud?

Credit card fraud occurs when a person uses another individual's credit card details without their permission to obtain goods, services, or money. This can happen when the physical card is stolen, or when only the card's information — such as the card number, expiration date, and CVV — is compromised. Fraudsters may use stolen card details to make purchases online or create counterfeit cards for in-person transactions. Under RA 10175 and the Access Devices Regulation Act of 1998 (RA 8484), credit card fraud is a punishable crime in the Philippines. Victims can face financial losses that take months to recover from, even with bank assistance. The emotional stress of dealing with unauthorized charges and disputes can also be significant. Banks and financial institutions invest heavily in fraud detection systems, but criminals continue to find new ways to bypass these safeguards.

Common Methods Used by Fraudsters

Credit card fraudsters use a wide range of techniques to steal card information from unsuspecting victims. Card skimming involves placing a hidden device on ATMs or point-of-sale terminals that reads and copies card data when the card is inserted or swiped. Phishing emails and fake websites trick users into entering their card details. Data breaches at online stores and payment processors expose millions of card records to hackers. Card-not-present fraud is especially common in online shopping, where only the card number, expiry date, and CVV are needed. "Carding" refers to testing stolen card details through small online purchases to see which cards still work. Physical theft of the card itself remains a simple but effective method. Social engineering, where criminals pose as bank agents and ask for card details, is also widely used.

Types of Credit Card Fraud

Credit card fraud can be categorized into several types based on how the card information is stolen and used. Lost or stolen card fraud occurs when someone physically obtains another person's card and uses it. Account takeover fraud involves a criminal gaining access to a cardholder's account and changing the contact details to lock out the real owner. New account fraud uses stolen personal information to apply for a credit card in someone else's name. Counterfeit card fraud uses skimmed data to create fake physical cards. CNP (Card Not Present) fraud is the most common type in online transactions, where the card does not need to be physically presented. Each type requires different countermeasures from both the cardholder and the bank.

Signs That Your Card May Have Been Compromised

There are several tell-tale signs that your credit card may have been used fraudulently. You may notice unfamiliar charges on your monthly statement, even small ones, as fraudsters often test cards with minor purchases before making large transactions. Receiving a new credit card in the mail that you did not apply for is a serious warning sign. Alerts from your bank about suspicious activity or declined transactions you did not initiate should not be ignored. Your bank may contact you to verify a purchase that seems out of the ordinary in terms of location or amount. Unexplained changes to your billing address or contact details are also red flags. Always review your bank statements carefully every month, even if you believe you have not been targeted. Acting quickly when you notice these signs can prevent further losses.

How to Protect Your Credit Card

Protecting your credit card requires a combination of careful habits and the use of security features available from your bank. Always cover the keypad when entering your PIN at ATMs or payment terminals. Only shop on websites that use HTTPS encryption and have a trusted reputation. Sign up for SMS or email alerts so your bank notifies you of every transaction in real-time. Never share your card details — including the CVV — over the phone or through email. Use a virtual card number for online purchases when your bank offers this option, as it protects your real card details. Check your credit card statements monthly and dispute any unauthorized charges immediately. If your card is lost or stolen, report it to your bank right away to have it blocked before it can be misused.

Conclusion

Credit card fraud is a serious and widespread crime that can cause significant financial and emotional harm to victims. Fraudsters use sophisticated methods such as skimming, phishing, and data breaches to steal card information. Different types of credit card fraud target different vulnerabilities, from physical card theft to complex online account takeovers. Recognizing the warning signs and taking proactive steps to secure your card can greatly reduce your risk. Staying alert and regularly reviewing your financial statements is one of the simplest but most effective defenses. Banks and consumers must work together to create a safer financial environment. Your money is worth protecting — make every transaction a conscious and secure one.
