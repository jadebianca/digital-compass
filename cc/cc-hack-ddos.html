Website Down: What You Need to Know About DDoS

By: MORALES, JOHN MIGUEL B. • MARCH 2026

Introduction • What Exactly is a DDoS Attack? • The "Botnet" Army: How Hackers Get Power • Why Do Cybercriminals Launch These Attacks? • DoS vs. DDoS: What is the Difference? • Real-Life Examples of DDoS Chaos • Warning Signs of a DDoS Attack • How Websites Defend Against the Flood • Conclusion

A distributed denial of service, or DDoS, assault is a type of cyberthreat that causes a website or online service to slow down or stop functioning by flooding it with excessive traffic. This may interfere with regular operations and prevent visitors from accessing the website. Depending on how severe they are, these episodes could go on for hours or even days. Such disruptions can cause major issues and losses because many businesses rely on their internet systems. DDoS assaults are frequently launched by attackers during significant events, which can harm company operations and can reveal security flaws.

What Exactly is a DDoS Attack?

A cybercrime known as a distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) assault occurs when a server is overloaded with internet traffic, making it impossible for users to access websites or online services. This attack interferes with regular business activities and has the potential to seriously harm both people and companies. DDoS assaults can be carried out for a variety of reasons, such as extortion, financial gain, political comments, or personal retaliation. While some attackers target nation-states with the intention of disrupting infrastructure, others target businesses to steal business or demand money to restore services. DDoS assaults are becoming more commonplace worldwide, impacting even major corporations like Amazon Web Services. They may lead to decreased revenue, traffic, and reputational damage. Protecting networks from DDoS attacks is now crucial due to an increase in linked devices and remote workers.

The "Botnet" Army: How Hackers Get Power

A botnet is a network of malware-infected devices that hackers use to launch coordinated attacks. A hacker known as a "bot herder" gives commands to each compromised device, referred to as a "bot" or "zombie." Any linked system is vulnerable to botnets, which can target phones, computers, and even Internet of Things devices. Hackers can conceal assaults by using peer-to-peer networks or command and control servers to direct bots without people seeing. These assaults have the potential to overwhelm systems, steal information, or propagate other malware to grow the botnet. By utilizing security software, keeping an eye on network activity, and updating software, users may safeguard their devices. By merging numerous compromised devices, botnets enable attackers to cause widespread harm.

Why Do Cybercriminals Launch These Attacks?

DDoS assaults are frequently launched by cybercriminals for financial gain, with the threat to halt the attack only upon payment. Some attacks are driven by competition, with the goal of upsetting competitors and obtaining a competitive edge. Attacks are also motivated by political or ideological factors; hacktivists want to harm governments, vital infrastructure, or organizations. While defenders concentrate on restoring services, DDoS attacks might serve as a distraction, concealing other cybercrimes like data breaches. Attacks that seek to harm or degrade organizations or businesses can also be motivated by revenge. Strong cybersecurity is crucial due to the increasing complexity of DDoS assaults. To lower risks and preserve network availability, businesses need to deploy detection and protection tools.

DoS vs. DDoS: What is the Difference?

A denial-of-service (DoS) attack prevents a website or service from operating by flooding a server with traffic from a single source. A distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) assault, on the other hand, targets a single system using numerous computers and generates a significantly higher volume of traffic. While DDoS attacks conceal their source among numerous devices, DoS attacks are simpler to identify and stop because they originate from a single spot. Additionally, DDoS attacks can overload servers more effectively than DoS attacks and happen more quickly. A botnet, in which a hacker controls compromised devices via a command-and-control server, is frequently used in DDoS attacks. DoS assaults often depend on a single tool or script. It is more challenging to track the origin of a DDoS attack than a DoS strike.

Real-Life Examples of DDoS Chaos

On October 21, 2016, the Mirai botnet was used to launch a massive DDoS attack on Dyn, a major DNS provider. The first wave overwhelmed Dyn’s systems with tens of millions of requests, making over 50 internet platforms, including PayPal, Twitter, Amazon, and Netflix, temporarily unavailable across the Northeastern U.S. and parts of Europe.y Dyn managed to restore services within two hours, though additional waves caused minor delays later in the day. Investigations later identified three college students as the creators of Mirai, who pleaded guilty to developing and sharing the botnet code. The attack caused significant business interruptions, with potential financial losses reaching thousands of dollars per minute of downtime. Recovery costs were also high, likely exceeding the average $2.5 million spent on DDoS recovery. Additionally, Dyn suffered reputational damage as some clients stopped using their services after the incident.

Warning Signs of a DDoS Attack

Because DDoS assaults mimic typical user traffic and appear to be ordinary technical issues, they are frequently difficult to identify. An abrupt increase in traffic from similar devices in the same area is one red flag. An attack may also be indicated by several requests from a single IP address in a brief amount of time. During ping testing, servers may timeout or send 503 errors, indicating that they are overcrowded. Unusual increases at odd times or targeting particular endpoints may be revealed via traffic analysis. Another sign is regular, unusual traffic patterns. Organizations can respond promptly and defend their networks against DDoS attacks by identifying these warning indicators.

How Websites Defend Against the Flood

Websites employ a variety of strategies to fend against and lessen DDoS attacks. Overload can be avoided by reducing the attack surface by restricting traffic from certain sites or shutting unneeded ports. By distributing traffic among several servers, Anycast networks enable websites to handle significant spikes without going offline. Unusual patterns are found by real-time network activity monitoring, allowing for prompt reaction to threats. Caching content using a content delivery network lessens the load on the primary servers by reducing requests. In order to avoid flooding, rate limitation regulates the quantity of requests from each IP. Before harmful traffic reaches servers, web application firewalls filter and stop it. Continuous protection and scalable defense against sophisticated attacks are offered by always-on DDoS mitigation services.

Conclusion

Cyberthreats known as distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) assaults overload websites or online services with excessive traffic, causing them to slow down or stop operating. These assaults can interfere with business operations for hours or even days, resulting in monetary losses, harm to a company's reputation, and decreased user access. Botnets, or networks of malware-infected devices, are frequently used by attackers to plan large-scale, difficult-to-trace attacks. Financial gain, political or ideological objectives, retaliation, or hiding additional cybercrimes are some of the motivations. Incidents from real life, like the Mirai botnet attack on Dyn, demonstrate the detrimental effects on companies and significant online platforms. Early detection of assaults can be aided by warning indicators such as anomalous traffic spikes and server problems. Monitoring, firewalls, caching, rate limiting, and always-on DDoS mitigation solutions are ways that organizations may protect themselves.
