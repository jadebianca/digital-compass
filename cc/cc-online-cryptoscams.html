Cryptocurrency Scams

By: Hamoy, Kurt Tobey • April 2026

Intro • What is Crypto Fraud • Types of Scams • How Scammers Operate • Red Flags • Staying Safe • Conclusion

Introduction

Cryptocurrency has become one of the most talked-about financial topics in recent years, and unfortunately, it has also become a hotbed for scams. Cryptocurrency scams are fraudulent schemes that trick people into investing in fake or manipulated digital currencies, resulting in significant financial losses. In the Philippines, interest in crypto has grown rapidly, but so has the number of Filipinos who have been victimized by crypto fraud. With promises of high returns and financial freedom, scammers lure in even financially savvy individuals. This article will help you understand how crypto scams work and how to protect yourself.

What is Cryptocurrency Fraud?

Cryptocurrency fraud refers to any deceptive scheme that uses digital currencies like Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other altcoins to steal money from victims. Unlike traditional bank transfers, cryptocurrency transactions are largely irreversible and anonymous, making it very difficult for victims to recover their funds. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) of the Philippines has issued warnings and cease-and-desist orders against multiple fake crypto investment platforms. Scammers exploit people's lack of knowledge about how blockchain and cryptocurrency work to make false claims about guaranteed profits. The decentralized nature of crypto makes it attractive to both legitimate investors and criminals alike. Once money is sent to a scammer's crypto wallet, it is nearly impossible to get back. The Philippine government has urged the public to only invest through SEC-registered and BSP-authorized platforms.

Common Types of Cryptocurrency Scams

There are many forms of cryptocurrency scams, each designed to exploit different human vulnerabilities. Rug pulls occur when developers of a new cryptocurrency collect investor funds and then suddenly abandon the project, leaving investors with worthless tokens. Pump and dump schemes involve artificially inflating the price of a coin through coordinated hype, then selling at the peak and letting the price crash. Fake exchanges impersonate legitimate crypto trading platforms and steal deposits. Romance scams involve building fake relationships online before convincing victims to invest in cryptocurrency. Giveaway scams promise to double your crypto if you send some first, often using fake celebrity endorsements. Ponzi schemes pay early investors using money from new investors until the scheme collapses. Each of these scams is designed to be convincing enough to fool even educated people.

How Crypto Scammers Target Their Victims

Cryptocurrency scammers are highly organized and use psychological tactics to manipulate their targets. They often approach victims through social media platforms, dating apps, or messaging groups, presenting themselves as financial experts or successful investors. They build trust over days or weeks before introducing the "investment opportunity." Scammers show victims fake dashboards with impressive returns to make the scheme look legitimate. Once the victim invests, they may even be allowed to "withdraw" small amounts at first to build confidence. When the victim invests a large amount, the scammers disappear or claim a technical issue prevents withdrawals. In some cases, they demand additional "taxes" or "fees" before allowing withdrawal — a final attempt to extract more money before vanishing.

Red Flags of a Crypto Scam

Recognizing the warning signs of cryptocurrency fraud can save you from potentially devastating financial losses. Be extremely wary of any platform that promises guaranteed high returns with no risk — no legitimate investment can guarantee profits, especially in the volatile crypto market. Unsolicited messages on social media or dating apps that quickly lead to investment advice should be treated with deep suspicion. Platforms that are not registered with the SEC or authorized by the BSP are operating illegally and should be avoided entirely. If you cannot withdraw your funds without paying additional fees, this is a classic sign of a scam. Pressure to recruit friends and family into the investment is a hallmark of Ponzi and pyramid schemes. Celebrity endorsements on social media for crypto platforms are almost always fake. Always research before investing and consult a licensed financial advisor.

How to Protect Yourself from Crypto Scams

Protecting yourself from cryptocurrency scams starts with education and skepticism toward extraordinary claims. Only invest in cryptocurrencies through SEC-registered and BSP-authorized platforms. Before investing, research the team, whitepaper, and use case behind any cryptocurrency project. Never invest more than you can afford to lose, as crypto markets are inherently volatile even under legitimate circumstances. Use hardware wallets or trusted software wallets with strong security to store your crypto assets safely. Be cautious of anyone online who claims to be a successful trader and offers to manage your investments. Report suspected crypto scam platforms to the SEC or BSP immediately. Share what you know about crypto scams with your family and friends, especially those who are new to digital investing.

Conclusion

Cryptocurrency scams are among the most financially devastating forms of online fraud, often leaving victims with no way to recover their money. From rug pulls and pump-and-dump schemes to fake exchanges and romance scams, criminals have found many clever ways to exploit interest in digital currency. The promise of high returns and the complexity of crypto technology make it easy for fraudsters to deceive even well-informed people. Staying informed, verifying platforms, and questioning too-good-to-be-true offers are the most effective defenses. Always invest only through authorized and legitimate platforms and never rush into financial decisions because of pressure. The Philippine SEC and BSP are valuable resources for checking whether a crypto platform is legally operating. Your financial security is worth more than any promised return.
