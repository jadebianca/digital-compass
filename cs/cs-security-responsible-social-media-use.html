Recognizing Suspicious Activity

By: LUIS MICHAEL L. SUING • April 8, 2026

Introduction • Urgency • Unexpected Links • Poor Grammar • Mismatched URLs • Sensitive Info • Account Activity • Device Issues • Conclusion

In the digital age, cyber threats are constantly evolving, becoming more sophisticated and harder to detect. However, no matter how advanced a scam or cyberattack may be, it usually leaves behind subtle clues. Learning to recognize these digital red flags is your first and most important line of defense against phishing, malware, and identity theft.

Urgent or Threatening Language

Cybercriminals often rely on human psychology to bypass your critical thinking. They frequently use urgent, threatening, or fear-inducing language to panic you into acting quickly. Emails claiming your account will be permanently deleted in 24 hours, or text messages stating you owe immediate legal fines, are classic hallmarks of a social engineering scam designed to make you click without thinking.

Unexpected Attachments and Links

If you receive an unsolicited email containing a link or an attachment—even if it appears to come from someone you know—treat it with extreme caution. Hackers routinely compromise legitimate email accounts to send malicious files to the victim's contact list. Invoices for items you didn't purchase or generic documents labeled "Urgent_Review.pdf" are common vectors for ransomware and spyware.

Generic Greetings and Poor Grammar

Legitimate organizations generally address you by your real name because they have it on file. Emails that start with "Dear Customer," "Valued Member," or "Dear [Email Address]" are highly suspicious. Additionally, while minor typos happen, persistent spelling mistakes, awkward phrasing, and poor grammar are strong indicators that the communication is originating from an unprofessional or foreign scam operation.

Mismatched Sender Addresses and URLs

Scammers are adept at making emails look official by stealing company logos and formatting. However, they cannot easily fake the actual sender's email address or the destination URL. Always hover your mouse over links (without clicking) to preview the real web address at the bottom of your browser. Also, scrutinize the sender's email—a message from "support@paypa1.com" instead of "paypal.com" is a clear trap.

Requests for Highly Sensitive Information

A legitimate bank, government agency, or service provider will almost never contact you out of the blue to ask for your password, Social Security number, or full credit card details over email or text message. If a message instructs you to reply with sensitive data or directs you to a login page to "verify your identity," it is almost certainly a phishing attempt.

Unusual Account Activity

Suspicious activity isn't just about what comes into your inbox; it's also about what happens inside your accounts. Red flags include receiving password reset emails you didn't request, discovering posts or messages on your social media that you didn't write, or finding unrecognized devices listed in your account's active sessions. These are signs that a hacker may have already gained access to your profile.

Unexplained Device Performance Issues

Sometimes the signs of suspicious activity are physical. If your computer or smartphone suddenly becomes incredibly slow, its battery drains unusually fast, or it runs hot when you aren't doing anything intensive, malicious software might be operating in the background. An increase in bizarre pop-up ads or changed browser homepages are also strong indicators of a malware or adware infection.

Conclusion

Staying safe online requires a healthy dose of skepticism. By pausing to evaluate the context, sender, and content of digital communications, you can spot the warning signs of a cyberattack before it does any damage. When in doubt, trust your instincts: avoid clicking, delete the suspicious message, and contact the supposed sender directly through their official website or customer service phone number to verify the claim.
