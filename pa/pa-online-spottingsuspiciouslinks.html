Spotting Suspicious Links

By: Cajayon, Hadley P. • March 28, 2026

Introduction • Why Links Are Dangerous • Warning Signs of a Suspicious Link • Understanding the Anatomy of a URL • The Danger of Shortened Links • Tools to Check Links Before Clicking • What To Do If You Clicked a Suspicious Link • Conclusion

One of the most common ways cybercriminals gain access to personal information, install malware, or hijack accounts is through malicious links. These links are designed to look harmless — or even trustworthy — but lead to dangerous websites that steal your data or infect your device. They arrive through emails, text messages, social media posts, online ads, and even in comments sections. Learning how to spot a suspicious link before clicking it is one of the simplest yet most powerful skills you can develop for your online safety.

Why Links Are Dangerous

A malicious link can cause serious harm in multiple ways. Phishing links direct you to fake websites that look identical to legitimate ones — such as your bank or a popular online store — and prompt you to enter your login credentials or payment details, which are then stolen. Drive-by download links automatically install malware on your device the moment you visit the page, without requiring you to click or download anything. Some links redirect you through multiple sites before landing on a malicious page, making it difficult to trace the origin. Others may simply track your behavior and sell your data to advertisers or cybercriminals.

Warning Signs of a Suspicious Link

There are several telltale signs that a link may be suspicious. Misspelled domain names are a common tactic — for example, "paypa1.com" instead of "paypal.com," or "g00gle.com" instead of "google.com." Look out for links that use hyphens to mimic legitimate sites, such as "facebook-login.com." URLs that begin with "http://" instead of "https://" lack encryption and should be treated with caution, especially on pages that ask for personal information. Unusually long or complex URLs filled with random characters are also red flags. Links that arrive in unsolicited messages — particularly those urging you to act quickly — should always be treated with extreme suspicion.

Understanding the Anatomy of a URL

Understanding how a URL is structured helps you identify suspicious ones. A URL typically consists of a protocol (https://), a domain name (google.com), and a path (/search?q=example). The most important part to examine is the domain name — specifically the part immediately before the first single slash. For example, in "https://secure.paypal.com/signin," the domain is "paypal.com," which is legitimate. But in "https://paypal.com.secure-login.xyz/signin," the domain is actually "secure-login.xyz" — a completely different, potentially malicious site. Always read URLs carefully from right to left after the "https://" to identify the true domain.

The Danger of Shortened Links

URL shorteners like bit.ly, tinyurl.com, and similar services compress long URLs into short ones, making them convenient to share — but also making it impossible to see where the link actually leads before clicking. Cybercriminals frequently use shortened URLs to disguise malicious links. Before clicking a shortened link, use a URL expander tool — such as checkshorturl.com or unshorten.it — to reveal the full destination URL. If you receive a shortened link unexpectedly from an unknown sender, it is safest to simply not click it at all.

Tools to Check Links Before Clicking

Several free tools can help you verify whether a link is safe before you click it. VirusTotal (virustotal.com) allows you to paste a URL and checks it against dozens of security databases to detect threats. Google Safe Browsing (safebrowsing.google.com) lets you check if a site has been flagged for phishing or malware. Norton Safe Web and URLVoid offer similar link-scanning services. Many modern web browsers and antivirus programs also include built-in link-checking features that warn you before you visit a potentially dangerous site. These tools take only seconds to use and can prevent significant harm.

What To Do If You Clicked a Suspicious Link

If you suspect you have clicked a malicious link, act quickly. Do not enter any personal information on the page that opened. Close the browser tab or window immediately. Run a full antivirus scan on your device to check for malware. If you entered any login credentials, change your passwords for those accounts immediately and enable two-factor authentication. If financial information was involved, notify your bank or financial institution as soon as possible. Report the suspicious link to the platform where you received it — whether that is an email provider, social media site, or messaging app — so others can be protected as well.

Conclusion

Suspicious links are one of the most widely used tools in a cybercriminal's arsenal, but they are also one of the easiest threats to defend against with the right knowledge. By slowing down before you click, examining URLs carefully, using link-checking tools, and knowing what to do if you make a mistake, you significantly reduce your vulnerability to phishing, malware, and data theft. Make it a habit to think before you click — that one second of caution could save you from a serious cybersecurity incident.
