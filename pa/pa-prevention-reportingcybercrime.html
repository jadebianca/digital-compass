Reporting Cybercrime

By: Cajayon, Hadley P. • April, 2026

Introduction • Why Report? • Where to Report • Gathering Evidence • Platform Reporting • After Reporting • Philippine Law • Conclusion

Many victims of cybercrime choose not to report what happened to them — out of embarrassment, fear, or the mistaken belief that nothing can be done. However, reporting cybercrime is one of the most important actions a victim can take. Not only does it open the possibility of recovering losses or holding perpetrators accountable, but it also helps authorities identify and track cybercriminal activity, ultimately protecting others from experiencing the same harm. Knowing where to report, what to say, and how to prepare makes the process less overwhelming and more effective.

Why Reporting Matters

When a cybercrime goes unreported, it effectively disappears from the records — leaving authorities without the data they need to respond to emerging threats. Every report, even if it does not lead directly to an arrest, contributes to a larger picture of cybercriminal behavior. Reports help law enforcement spot patterns, issue public warnings, and coordinate with international agencies when criminals operate across borders. Reporting also protects other potential victims: for example, reporting a phishing website can lead to its takedown before more people are deceived. In short, reporting is an act of community protection, not just personal justice.

Where to Report in the Philippines

In the Philippines, there are several official channels for reporting cybercrime. The Philippine National Police Anti-Cybercrime Group (PNP-ACG) accepts reports through their hotline, walk-in offices, and online complaint form at acg.pnp.gov.ph. The National Bureau of Investigation Cybercrime Division (NBI-CCD) is another option, especially for more serious offenses. The Cybercrime Investigation and Coordinating Center (CICC), under the Department of Information and Communications Technology (DICT), also serves as a coordinating body for cybercrime cases and can be reached at 1326. For scams involving bank accounts or financial fraud, reports can also be filed with the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) or the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

Gathering Evidence Before You Report

Before making a report, gather and preserve as much evidence as possible. Take screenshots of suspicious messages, emails, or websites — including the sender's address, timestamps, and any linked URLs. Save transaction records, receipts, or chat logs if financial fraud is involved. Do not delete any messages even if they seem unimportant, as investigators may find details useful that you might overlook. If you received a phishing link, do not click it — instead, copy the URL and include it in your report. Organizing your evidence clearly before reporting will help investigators process your case more efficiently.

Reporting on Online Platforms

Most social media and digital platforms have built-in mechanisms to report suspicious content, accounts, or messages. On Facebook, you can report a post, profile, or message directly using the three-dot menu or flag icon. Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, and YouTube have similar features. For e-commerce platforms like Shopee or Lazada, there are dispute resolution and seller report options. Reporting suspicious content on these platforms not only flags the account for review but also helps the platform's security team act faster. These reports are separate from — and should not replace — official law enforcement reports when a crime has occurred.

What Happens After You Report

After filing a report, authorities will typically acknowledge receipt and assign a case number. Investigations may take time, especially if the case involves anonymous actors or cross-border operations. You may be asked to provide additional information or appear for follow-up interviews. Not every report results in an arrest, but they all matter. In cases involving financial loss, you should also notify your bank immediately to freeze or monitor your account, and change your passwords and security settings as a precaution. Keeping a record of your report, including the date, reference number, and contact person, is helpful for any follow-up.

The Philippine Cybercrime Prevention Act

The legal backbone for cybercrime reporting and prosecution in the Philippines is Republic Act No. 10175, also known as the Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012. This law defines and penalizes a wide range of cyber offenses, including hacking, online fraud, cybersex, child pornography, and identity theft. Under this act, law enforcement agencies are empowered to investigate, collect digital evidence, and prosecute offenders. Victims are afforded certain protections, and offenders can face imprisonment and substantial fines. Knowing your legal rights as a victim empowers you to take action with confidence.

Conclusion

Reporting cybercrime is not just a personal decision — it is a civic responsibility. Every report helps build a safer digital environment for everyone. Whether you were scammed online, had your account hacked, or witnessed suspicious activity, the right thing to do is to speak up through the proper channels. The process may seem daunting at first, but the resources and authorities are there to help. Do not let fear or embarrassment stop you from taking action. Your report could be the key that stops a criminal from harming the next person.
