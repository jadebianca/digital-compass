Protecting Devices

By: Cajayon, Hadley P. • March 28, 2026

Introduction • Keep Software Updated • Use Antivirus Software • Strong Passwords & 2FA • Wi-Fi & Network Safety • Safe Downloads • Backup Your Data • Conclusion

Our devices are among our most valuable possessions in the digital age. Smartphones, laptops, tablets, and computers store personal photos, financial records, private conversations, and login credentials — making them prime targets for cybercriminals. Protecting your devices is not a one-time task; it is an ongoing commitment to safe digital habits. Fortunately, most of the best protection strategies are simple, free or low-cost, and easy to implement. This article outlines the key steps everyone should take to keep their devices — and the sensitive data stored on them — secure.

Keep Your Software Updated

One of the simplest yet most effective ways to protect your device is to keep your operating system and applications up to date. Software updates are not just about new features — they often include critical security patches that fix vulnerabilities discovered by developers or security researchers. Cybercriminals frequently exploit outdated software because the weaknesses are publicly known. Enabling automatic updates on your devices ensures you receive these patches as soon as they are released, without having to remember to check manually. This applies to your phone, computer, web browser, and any other apps you use regularly.

Install Antivirus and Anti-Malware Software

Antivirus and anti-malware programs serve as a frontline defense against malicious software that can steal your data, spy on your activity, or hold your files hostage. These programs scan your device for known threats, block suspicious activity in real time, and alert you when something dangerous is detected. Many reputable options are available for free, including Windows Defender (built into Windows) and free versions of Malwarebytes. For stronger protection, paid versions with additional features like web browsing protection and email scanning are worth considering. Run regular full system scans — especially after downloading new files or visiting unfamiliar websites.

Use Strong Passwords and Enable Two-Factor Authentication

Weak or reused passwords are one of the leading causes of account breaches. A strong password should be at least 12 characters long and include a combination of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. Avoid using easily guessable information like your name, birthday, or common words. Because remembering many complex passwords is difficult, using a trusted password manager — such as Bitwarden or LastPass — allows you to generate and store unique passwords for every account securely. Additionally, enabling Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) adds a vital extra layer of security: even if someone obtains your password, they cannot access your account without the second verification step, such as a code sent to your phone.

Practice Wi-Fi and Network Safety

Public Wi-Fi networks — in cafes, malls, airports, and schools — are convenient but inherently risky. These networks are often unsecured, meaning anyone on the same network may be able to intercept your data. Avoid accessing sensitive accounts like online banking or email while connected to public Wi-Fi. If you must use public Wi-Fi, use a Virtual Private Network (VPN), which encrypts your internet connection and shields your activity from prying eyes. At home, secure your Wi-Fi router with a strong password, change the default admin credentials, and use WPA3 or WPA2 encryption. Regularly check which devices are connected to your home network to spot any unauthorized access.

Be Careful What You Download

Malware is often hidden inside seemingly harmless files — a free game, a PDF attachment, a software crack, or a plugin. Only download apps and software from official sources: the Apple App Store, Google Play Store, or the developer's official website. Be cautious of email attachments from unknown senders, even if the file appears to be a common type like a Word document or ZIP file. Before opening any downloaded file, scan it with your antivirus software. Avoid pirated software not only because it is illegal, but because it is a common vehicle for malware. If a download source feels suspicious, trust that instinct and look for a safer alternative.

Back Up Your Data Regularly

Even with all the right protections in place, no device is completely immune to failure or attack. Ransomware, for example, encrypts your files and demands payment to restore access — but if you have a recent backup, you can simply restore your data without paying the ransom. Follow the 3-2-1 backup rule: keep three copies of your data, on two different types of storage media, with one copy stored offsite or in the cloud. Cloud services like Google Drive, OneDrive, or iCloud offer convenient automatic backups for phones and computers. External hard drives or USB drives work well for local backups. Schedule backups to run automatically and test your backup files periodically to make sure they work.

Conclusion

Protecting your devices is one of the most tangible and impactful things you can do for your digital safety. The steps outlined here — keeping software updated, using antivirus tools, creating strong passwords, staying safe on networks, downloading carefully, and backing up data — form a comprehensive defense against the most common cyber threats. None of these habits require advanced technical knowledge; they simply require consistency and intention. Start with one step today, and build from there. Your devices, your data, and your peace of mind are worth the effort.
